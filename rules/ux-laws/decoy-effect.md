## The Rule
- Use a decoy: an option dominated by the target but not by the competitor.
- Position it so the target looks relatively better without changing either option's objective value.
- The effect is asymmetric dominance: the decoy is clearly worse than the target on at least one dimension, so the target 'wins' the direct comparison.
- Classic example: small coffee $3, medium coffee $5, large coffee $5.50. Medium is the decoy. Large suddenly looks like high value.
- The effect works because humans evaluate options relatively, not absolutely.
- The decoy provides a reference point that favors the target.

## Why
- Ariely, Loewenstein, and Prelec (2003) demonstrated that preferences are constructed at decision time rather than retrieved from stable internal values, making them susceptible to context manipulation.
- Huber, Payne, and Puto (1982) established the core finding: introduction of a dominated alternative increases the market share of the dominating option by 10-15% on average.
- Loss aversion amplifies the effect: choosing the decoy over the target feels like a loss, nudging users toward the target.

## When It Breaks
- Experienced users who have researched the category independently are less susceptible.
- B2B procurement teams with formal evaluation criteria are harder to influence.
- If the decoy is too obvious (e.g., a plan with half the features for 90% of the price), users recognize the manipulation and distrust the interface.
- When all three options are equally attractive, no asymmetric dominance exists and the decoy has no directional effect.

## In Practice
- SaaS pricing: position the decoy as the middle tier. Make it worse than the Pro plan on storage or seats, while pricing it at 70-80% of Pro. Users perceive Pro as high value by comparison. Label the target with 'Most Popular' to reinforce the social signal.

## Key Numbers
- **10-15%** — Average market share shift to target (Huber et al. 1982)
