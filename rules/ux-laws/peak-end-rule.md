## The Rule
- Design the emotional peak and the ending deliberately. These two moments determine remembered experience quality, weighted almost equally.
- Duration neglect: longer experiences are not remembered as better or worse than shorter ones with the same peak and end.
- The rule applies to both positive and negative experiences.
- Redelmeier and Kahneman (1996): colonoscopy patients remembered procedures as less painful when discomfort decreased gradually at the end, even when total duration was longer.
- Net Promoter Scores and satisfaction ratings correlate with peak and final touchpoints, not with average experience quality.

## Why
- Kahneman, Fredrickson, Schreiber, and Redelmeier (1993) established the peak-end rule with cold-water pain experiments.
- Subjects preferred a longer painful trial that included a mild tail-off to a shorter identical trial without one.
- The experiencing self (real-time emotional state) and the remembering self (retrospective judgment) operate independently.
- Design affects both differently.
- Memory is reconstructive.
- The brain encodes salient moments as anchors and interpolates the rest, making peak moments disproportionately weighted.

## When It Breaks
- Repeated, routine tasks (daily login, recurring data entry): users have extensive experience and accurate memory. The peak-end effect is weaker when multiple experiences average out.
- Products evaluated by committees or on checklists: rational feature evaluation overrides emotional memory.
- Very short interactions (under 10 seconds): there may be insufficient time to establish a salient peak distinct from the end.

## In Practice
- Onboarding: design the 'aha moment' as an explicit peak (e.g., the first successful action with a celebratory animation), and end onboarding with a clear, positive confirmation screen summarizing what was set up. Never end with a form or an error state. Duolingo's post-lesson XP animation and Slack's channel setup confirmation are applied examples.
