## The Rule
- Text-based marks: wordmark (full brand name in a set typeface), lettermark (initials only).
- Symbol-based marks: pictorial mark (recognizable subject), abstract mark (geometric form with no inherent meaning), emblem (text inside a containing shape).
- Combination mark: any symbol-based mark paired with a wordmark. The most common primary lockup.
- A brand system typically contains three lockups: primary (combination mark), secondary (wordmark or mark), minimum (symbol or lettermark). Design convention, not a formal standard.
- Use the combination mark by default. Switch to the mark alone at sizes where the wordmark becomes illegible.
- Emblems require a dedicated simplified variant for small contexts. The text and shape are inseparable by design.

## Why
- At small sizes, fine letterforms and symbol detail fall below the minimum legible stroke width for screen rendering.
- Pictorial marks carry inherent subject meaning (an apple, a bird). Abstract marks carry no inherent meaning and acquire it entirely through repeated brand exposure.
- A single fixed lockup at all sizes forces a trade-off: either too complex at small sizes, or too spacious at large ones.

## When It Breaks
- High-recognition wordmark brands (Coca-Cola, FedEx): the wordmark alone can serve larger size contexts. A minimum lockup (letterform or simplified mark) is still needed at favicon and app icon scale.
- Abstract marks require longer wordmark pairing than pictorial marks before standing alone, because they carry no inherent meaning independent of brand exposure.

## In Practice
- App icon: symbol or abstract mark only, wordmark omitted. Favicon: symbol or lettermark at small scale. Page header: combination mark. Social media avatar: symbol or abstract mark only.
