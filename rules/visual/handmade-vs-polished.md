## The Rule
- Polished design: precise geometry, clean type, consistent spacing, no visible hand. Signals professionalism, precision, and institutional credibility.
- Handmade design: irregular strokes, organic shapes, rough textures, visible grain. Signals human presence, authenticity, and effort.
- Neither is a quality signal on its own. Context determines which register builds trust with a specific audience.
- The distinction must be intentional. Accidental imperfection reads as incompetence. Deliberate imperfection reads as character.

## Why
- Nielsen Norman Group (2024): handmade aesthetics have become a trust signal. Consumers increasingly read high polish as potentially AI-generated or corporate, reducing perceived authenticity.
- The mechanism is inference: irregularity signals human involvement, which signals individual care.

## When It Breaks
- Finance, healthcare, and legal contexts: users expect precision. Handmade aesthetics in these domains create doubt about reliability.
- A poorly executed rough aesthetic reads as unfinished rather than authentic. High craft skill is required to make imperfection look intentional.
- Accessibility cannot be traded for texture. Irregular typography and low-contrast grain patterns frequently fail WCAG 2.2 contrast requirements.

## In Practice
- Artisan brand: use brush-stroke letterforms, off-white backgrounds with subtle paper grain (5-8% opacity noise overlay), and hand-drawn icon outlines with slightly variable stroke weight.
- SaaS dashboard: keep polished. Clean sans-serif, 1px borders, 4px border-radius, neutral gray palette. Reserve handmade elements for onboarding illustrations only.
